PANDA: Robot toy to detect neuorodevelopmental delays in infants

Rehabilitation Robotics Lab, University of Pennsylvania

Introduction
About 5-10 % of infants are born with neurodevelopmental disabilities such as Cerebral Palsy(CP). Such disabilities present as an array of cognitive, perception, and motor impairments. Rehabilitation has been shown to be especially effective before 2 years of age, due to the high neuroplasticity seen in infants. In order for this early intervention to occur, it is imperative that impairment is detected during this small window of development. Combining natural play with quantitative measurements of developmental milestones can be an effective way to swiftly and efficiently detect infants who are at risk of developmental delays. Clinical studies have established differences in toy interaction behaviors between healthy infants and those at risk for developmental delays. The proposed smart toy aims to enhance the quantitative assessment of infant toy interactions in order to detect risks of developing motor delays.
Working
The toy has lossy sensors, which are 3D printed lattices made from soft resin through which optical fibers are passed. Light from an LED comes in through one end of the optical fiber, and is detetced by a photodiode at the other end. When the sensor is compressed, the optical fibre bends which scatters the light. The amount of light scattered is co-related to the force exerted on the sensor.

My contributions

Smart Toy Designing

The face and body of the toy was designed to hold the lossy sensors, electrical components and the PCB, while keeping the size of the toy ideal for infants to play with. Slots were strategically incorporated into the face to house the sensors, which were instrumental in capturing data and facilitating interaction. Holders, placed in their respective slots, were designed to secure the LED and photodiode pairs in place. These holders play a crucial role in ensuring the accurate positioning and alignment of the LED and photodiode with the optical fibers. All of the parts were designed using Onshape and assembled in SolidWorks.

Smart Toy Development

3D printing was the primary process used for fabrication of the toy.

Component Material 3D Printer
Face ABS Stratasys F120 FDM
PCB Housing ABS Stratasys F120 FDM
Lattices Flexible 80A Resin Formlabs Form 3B+
LED, Photodiode Holders Flexible 80A Resin Formlabs Form 3B+

The 3D printed lattices and holders were fixed in their respective slots using hot glue. LEDs and Photodiodes were soldered to jumper cables, which were soldered on the main PCB. The PCB was placed inside the PCB Housing and the entire toy was then wrapped with cloth.

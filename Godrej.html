WATERTIGHT HATCH USED ON SURFACE SHIPS

Security Solutions Division, Godrej & Boyce

Introduction
Watertight doors (WTDs) are installed to prevent the ingress of water from one compartment to another during flooding. They are doorways to facilitate the movement of crew or goods in the ship. They are usually located at the bottom part of the ship where the engines and shaft tunnel are found. They may be kept close especially when the ship is underway or may be left open except during an emergency. They have to be designed so as to withstand the pressure of water and not allow any seepage from either side of the door.

My contributions

Designing and Analyzing Outer Structure

The initial phase involved meticulous modeling of the coaming, integrating a C-channel for additional support and designing hinge joints to mount the cover. Employing Creo Parametric CAD software, I predominantly utilized the Extrude feature. Subsequently, the cover was crafted using the shell feature, with matching hinges and strategically placed supports for interlocking capabilities. Initial structural analysis using Ansys revealed stress and deformation concerns, prompting the addition of stiffeners. The iterative process of stiffener design and analysis was pursued to optimize the structural integrity. Through multiple iterations, culminating in Stiffener 3, the maximum stress was successfully reduced to 138 MPa, well within acceptable limits.

Designing and Assembling Locking Mechanism

The focus shifted to implementing a locking mechanism for the hatch. I meticulously modeled separate locking dogs with cylindrical bushings, incorporating linkages to facilitate synchronized opening and closing. This mechanism, operationalized through a door handle, exhibited seamless functionality, ensuring reliable and efficient hatch operation.

Gas Spring Selection

To address the weight and facilitate the opening and closing process, gas springs from SUSPA, specifically the Gas Spring 16-4 model, were selected and integrated into the assembly. Calculations based on a safety factor of 2 resulted in the use of three gas springs, each capable of supporting 116.67 kg. Finally, the design was completed with the addition of a link to hold the cover in the open position, featuring a carefully designed pin mechanism for secure fastening. This comprehensive design and analysis process equipped me with invaluable practical insights into the intricate world of mechanical engineering and its real-world applications.
